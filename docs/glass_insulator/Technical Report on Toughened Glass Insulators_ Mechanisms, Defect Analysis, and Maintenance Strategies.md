## **1\. Functional and Physical Foundations of Glass Insulators**

In high-voltage transmission line operations, insulators serve a dual role: providing electrical insulation between conductors and supports while acting as mechanical supports capable of withstanding the weight of wires and various external pressures. Within these requirements, toughened glass insulators have become essential components of major power grids worldwide due to their unique reliability and ease of maintenance.  
The fundamental reason glass exhibits excellent insulation performance in high-voltage environments lies in its atomic structure and electronic bonding. Unlike copper, where valence electrons form a 'sea of electrons' free to move, glass features covalent bonds where valence electrons are strongly confined to specific positions. The amorphous structure of glass, with its irregularly arranged atoms, extremely raises the energy required for electron movement, leading to very high electrical resistance. Thanks to these physical foundations, typical toughened glass insulators can withstand an electrical tension of approximately 20 kV per unit thickness (1 mm), and standard models possess the ability to stably block voltages exceeding 200 kV.  
The mechanical strength of toughened glass insulators is determined by the heat treatment—or toughening process—during manufacturing. Heating the glass near its softening point and then rapidly cooling it forms a strong compressive stress layer (Skin) approximately 2 mm thick on the surface, while tensile stress remains internally. This equilibrium of stresses suppresses crack propagation when external impacts occur, providing mechanical impact strength about four times higher than that of porcelain insulators. Furthermore, toughened glass exhibits almost no aging phenomena and has excellent ultraviolet (UV) resistance, ensuring long-term reliability.

## **2\. Component Structure and Material Characteristics**

A glass insulator is a precisely designed mechanical connector rather than a simple insulator. The typical structure of a suspension (cap and pin) glass insulator consists of a glass shell (the insulating part), a metal cap, a steel pin, and cement to bond them.

### **Table 1: Main Components and Material Specifications**

| Component | Main Material | Characteristics and Functions   |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Glass Shell | Low-alkali toughened glass | Acts as the primary insulator; blocks electrical flow through an amorphous structure and ensures tensile strength via thermal toughening. |
| Cap | High-strength cast iron | A movable fixing device secured via hot-dip galvanization for corrosion resistance; connects to upper insulators or tower fittings. |
| Pin | Forged steel | The core axis bearing the load; hot-dip galvanized and may include a zinc sleeve to prevent electrolytic corrosion. |
| Cement | Alumina/Portland cement | Fills gaps between the glass shell and metal parts to distribute mechanical loads evenly and maintain strong bonding. |
| Locking Device | Stainless steel | A split pin that prevents separation after string assembly, ensuring stability under vibration or environmental loads. |

The shape of the glass shell is designed as Standard, Fog/Anti-fog, or Ultra Anti-fog based on the pollution level of the installation environment. Fog-type designs feature deep ribs at the bottom to increase the creepage distance, preventing flashover accidents in humid or coastal areas. For DC transmission, special glass formulations utilizing the 'Bi-alkali Effect' are applied to minimize corrosion and electrolytic erosion caused by leakage currents.

## **3\. Manufacturing Process and Quality Control**

The manufacturing of toughened glass insulators starts with batching raw materials, followed by melting, pressing, and toughening. Uniform mixing of raw materials is key to determining chemical stability, and the fining process during melting removes internal bubbles to reduce the risk of dielectric breakdown.  
The toughening process involves spraying air jets onto the surface of a glass shell heated to its softening point (760\~780°C) for rapid cooling. In this stage, the surface solidifies immediately, while the interior cools slowly and attempts to contract, establishing a permanent stress equilibrium. During assembly, the expansion coefficient of the cement must be precisely matched with the glass and metal, and after assembly, the strength of the cement mortar is maximized through temperature and humidity treatments. Quality inspections involve tensile load tests at 50% of the design load, structural height measurements, and thermal shock tests for all units.

## **4\. Installation Standards and System Design**

The installation of toughened glass insulators is based on the international standard IEC 60383, which power utilities adapt to their specific grid environments.

### **Table 2: Mechanical Load Ratings and Application Range**

| Load Rating (kN) | Primary Voltage and Environment | Remarks   |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 70kN \~ 100kN | 11kV – 33kV distribution and light-load lines | Standard load conditions |
| 120kN \~ 160kN | 66kV – 220kV high-voltage transmission and dead-end towers | High mechanical tension sections |
| 210kN \~ 300kN | 345kV – 1000kV UHV transmission and heavy ice/snow areas | Extreme environments and long spans |

According to KEPCO standards, 154kV lines typically use 10-11 units per string, 345kV lines use 18-23 units, and 765kV lines use even more units in V-suspension or I-suspension configurations. During installation, insulator strings must maintain a precise vertical or designed angle to prevent glass breakage from non-uniform stress concentration. Split pins must be fully inserted to prevent displacement due to vibration.

## **5\. Damage Causes and the Self-shattering Mechanism**

The most characteristic failure mode of toughened glass insulators is 'self-shattering,' where the glass shell breaks without external impact. This occurs when the internal stress balance is disrupted, caused by material defects or environmental stress.

* **Nickel Sulfide (NiS) Inclusion:** During manufacturing, trace amounts of nickel can combine with sulfur to form NiS particles. These particles exist in a stable alpha-phase at high temperatures but can remain in the glass after rapid cooling without transitioning. Over time, these particles slowly transition to the stable beta-phase at room temperature, expanding in volume by 2-4%. If this occurs in the tensile stress zone, the expansion exceeds the glass's strength, leading to immediate shattering.  
* **Thermal Stress:** Rapid temperature changes, such as in desert areas with high diurnal ranges or sudden rain, create stress imbalances between the surface and interior.  
* **Pollution and Leakage Current:** Salt or dust deposits combined with moisture create leakage currents, generating heat and forming dry bands. Arcing at these bands locally heats and erodes the glass, breaking the stress balance.  
* **External Impact:** Scratches from falling rocks, bird strikes, or careless tool use by maintenance personnel can destroy the compressive stress layer, triggering shattering.

## **6\. Analysis and Impact of Stubs (Missing Caps)**

A 'stub' or 'missing cap' refers to the state where the glass shell has shattered and disappeared, leaving only the metal cap, pin, and glass fragments embedded in the cap. This is a safety design feature unique to glass insulators.  
Research confirms that shattered stubs retain at least 80% of their original design load capacity, supporting lines even under severe tension for 500kV systems. The conical design inside the cap causes glass fragments to act like a wedge as tensile load increases, strengthening the bond. While insulation and creepage distances are reduced, a dry stub possesses a flashover voltage of approximately 12kV (AC). This prevents catastrophic accidents like cap explosions or line drops by allowing external flashovers rather than internal punctures.

## **7\. Defect Classification and Diagnostic Systems**

The diagnosis of glass insulators is primarily based on visual inspection, complemented by advanced non-destructive techniques.

### **Table 3: Classification of Major Defect Types**

| Defect Category | Main Phenomena and Symptoms | Diagnostic Method   |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Self-shattering (Stub) | Loss of glass shell; only cap/pin remain. Immediately identifiable. | Visual inspection, UAV (Drone) aerial photography . |
| Surface Pollution | Salt or dust buildup. Sizzling noise when wet. | UV camera (Corona), leakage current measurement. |
| Cement Deterioration | Cement expansion/cracking due to Alkali-Silica Reaction (ASR). | Ultrasonic diagnosis, 3D-CT analysis. |
| Metal Corrosion | Galvanization loss and rust formation. | Telescope visual check, precise UAV photography. |
| Electrical Deterioration | Drop in insulation resistance (Zero-value); field distortion. | Megger, field strength measurement devices. |

Advanced techniques such as Drone and AI-based detection use deep learning algorithms (e.g., Faster R-CNN) to automatically identify stubs. Infrared (IR) thermography identifies faulty insulators through abnormal temperature distributions, while electric field analysis detects exact defect locations via field strength fluctuations.

## **8\. Maintenance Strategies and Replacement Standards**

The timing for replacing defective glass insulators is determined by balancing system stability and economic efficiency.

* **Quantitative Criteria:** Using Weibull distribution analysis, entire strings are recommended for replacement if the failure probability $P(F)$ exceeds a threshold (e.g., 4.42%).  
* **Aging Criteria:** Mechanical strength drops significantly 30 years after installation; units over 50 years old have a 2.7% higher probability of failing below design loads, necessitating preemptive replacement.  
* **Operational Methods:** Stubs can often be replaced during scheduled maintenance rather than immediate emergency shutdowns, as they maintain mechanical strength. For critical lines, live-line maintenance using hot sticks or robots is employed, maintaining safe clearances (e.g., 1.6m for 154kV, 3.5m for 345kV).

## **9\. Conclusion and Technical Recommendations**

Toughened glass insulators provide high mechanical reliability and maintenance convenience through their physical structure and toughening mechanisms. Self-shattering, while a unique failure mode, acts as a 'safety device' by making defects clearly visible, preventing major accidents caused by hidden internal flaws. Operators should build integrated diagnostic systems considering environmental factors like pollution and temperature rather than treating shattering solely as material failure. The integration of smart insulators with real-time leakage current sensors is expected to further enhance preventive maintenance efficiency.
